In the 17th and 18th centuries many people left the farms and moved to cities, usually for economic reasons.
One of the earliest was John Woolcott, citizen and guard of London, who died there in 1626. His will mentions only a wife and sister, so probably left no heirs.
Robert Woolcott, probably son of Robert Woolcott of Wiveliscombe, Somerset, came to London where he married his wife, Bridget about 1640 and had 6 children. Only one, Daniel Woolcott of St. Botolph, Aldgate, b. 1651, had children. His son, Robert, was an tailor and paid land taxes in Aldgate from 1705 until his death. He had 10 children, one of which, Robert, was a sadler and admitted as citizen of London in 1723. See Wolcotts of Somerset.

John Woolcott, 1756-1825, of Crewkerne, Somerset and his sons, John (1793-c.1831) and Philip (1797-1835), operated a transportation and freight hauling business between London and Southwest England. He was called a "carrier" in 1787. In 1791 he kept "The Rose and Crown" Inn in Crewkerne where he caried frieght between Crewkerne and Salisbury. He took in a partner the following year to run a freight line from Salisbury to London. In 1790-1793 he resided at and operated the New Inn and Tavern in Sidmouth. By 1804 he had ninety horses and numerous wagons, using inns as his receiving houses. One of his properties was the New Inn in Sherborne, where he lived in 1811 and from where he kept warehouses and supervised his London to Exeter & Plymouthcarrier business. In 1815-1816 he held The Castle and Falcon Inn in Aldersgate, London, and in 1819-1825 the Swan Inn Yard in Holborn, and properties in Taunton. In 1825 John Woolcott was proprietor of the Elephant Inn in Fore St., London. He wanted to horse only from London to the first stage at Gerrard Cross, 23 miles from London, but was pursuaded to do so as far as Oxford. An agreement was made in 1825 for Woolcott to work 3 seven horse wagons a week between London and Oxford. He had his own staff at London for horsekeeping, loading and bookeeping. His 1826 willl left properties in Wiltshire, Hampshire, Dorset, Somerset and elsewhere, worth £3,000 in trust for his children, and if his sons wished to be carriers they to have what they need as part of their inheritance.
In 1823, his sons purchased and operated the Elephant Inn in Cripplegate, London. with 58 horses and 12 wagons, serving primarily Sherborne and Exeter. In 1825, when their father died, Philip and John, disolved the partnership and sold out, receiving £1800 for the stock and £1200 for the goodwill, a goodly sum in those days. John then operated a carrier business from Salisbury, where in 1832 he had 36 horses serving the Salisbury area. See Wolcotts of Somerset.
